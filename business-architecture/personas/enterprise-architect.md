# Persona: Enterprise Architect (Central IT)

**Validation Status: Assumed** — drafted without direct user research. Must not drive implementation until validated through interviews with real central IT enterprise architects in a state or local government context.

## Role Type
Internal — Central IT / Shared Services

## Who They Are
The Enterprise Architect works in a central IT organization (e.g., a state's Office of Information Technology, or a city's central IS department) and is responsible for maintaining an enterprise-wide view of business capabilities, shared services, and the application portfolio across all agencies. They set standards, identify duplication, and advise on investment decisions. They do not manage individual agency systems — they maintain the enterprise architecture reference that agencies connect to.

## Goals
- Publish enterprise-wide capabilities and personas that agencies can adopt and link to
- See which agencies are implementing which enterprise capabilities and how
- Identify capability duplication across agencies to make consolidation or shared-service recommendations
- Maintain an authoritative enterprise capability map that stays current as agencies evolve their portfolios
- Give agency architects a clear target to align to without imposing a top-down mandate

## Pain Points
- No visibility into what agencies are actually building or buying — decisions get made without central awareness
- Enterprise architecture artifacts (if they exist) live in documents or tools no agency actually uses
- No way to tell which agencies have addressed a shared need vs. which are still doing it independently
- Agencies are reluctant to share because they fear loss of control or being overruled
- Any tool that feels like a compliance or reporting burden gets abandoned quickly

## Critical Insight
The Enterprise Architect cannot mandate adoption. They earn relevance by publishing enterprise capabilities that agencies find genuinely useful as a reference — not as a checklist. The connection between agency and enterprise must feel like a professional network, not a audit. If agencies fear losing autonomy by connecting, they won't connect.

## Relevant Capabilities
- Publishing enterprise-wide capabilities and personas at instance level
- Aggregated view of connected agencies' shared capabilities
- Cross-org capability linking and approval
- Org connections management
